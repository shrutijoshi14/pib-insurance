Fire Insurance

Fire Insurance is one of the most fundamental protections any property owner can carry — and one of the most commonly undervalued. A fire event, even a contained one, can cause structural damage, destroy stock, and bring operations to a halt. With the right coverage in place, the financial cost of recovery is transferred to the insurer, not absorbed by your business.

What's Covered

  • Core fire coverage: direct damage from fire, lightning, and explosion — the most frequent and costly property risk events.
  • Allied perils (optional riders): storm, cyclone, flood, earthquake, riots, strikes, impact damage, and bursting of water tanks or pipes.
  • Applicable to any property type: homes, shops, offices, factories, warehouses, and industrial premises.
  • Reinstatement or market value basis: choose how your property is valued for the purpose of claim settlement.
  • Annual or long-term policy: standard one-year terms with long-term options available for residential buildings.
  • Bundling options: can be combined with burglary, theft, and business interruption covers to build broader protection.
  • SFSP policy framework: the Standard Fire & Special Perils structure is universally recognized by lenders, courts, and regulatory bodies.

Why It Matters

Financial recovery without depleting reserves: repair and rebuild costs are covered rather than funded from your operating capital.
Meets lender requirements: fire insurance is mandatory on most mortgaged and hypothecated properties — keeping you compliant with your financing agreements.
High coverage value for low premium: fire insurance typically offers one of the strongest coverage-to-cost ratios available in commercial insurance.
Resume operations faster: claim settlement funds restoration work rather than stalling it — minimizing downtime.
Build on it as you grow: allied perils and business interruption riders can be added progressively as your risk profile evolves.

Who Should Consider This

Business owners operating from owned or leased premises, manufacturers with fixed assets, retailers, and property owners with active mortgages.
